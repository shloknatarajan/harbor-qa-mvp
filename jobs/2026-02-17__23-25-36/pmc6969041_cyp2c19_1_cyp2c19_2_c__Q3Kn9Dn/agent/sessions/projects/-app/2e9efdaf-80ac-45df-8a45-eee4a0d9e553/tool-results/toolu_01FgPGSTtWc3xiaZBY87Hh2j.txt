     1→# Germline cis variant determines epigenetic regulation of the anti-cancer drug metabolism gene dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPYD)

    13→## Abstract
    14→
    15→Enhancers are critical for regulating tissue-specific gene expression, and genetic variants within enhancer regions have been suggested to contribute to various cancer-related processes, including therapeutic resistance. However, the precise mechanisms remain elusive. Using a well-defined drug-gene pair, we identified an enhancer region for dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPD, DPYD gene) expression that is relevant to the metabolism of the anti-cancer drug 5-fluorouracil (5-FU). Using reporter systems, CRISPR genome-edited cell models, and human liver specimens, we demonstrated in vitro and vivo that genotype status for the common germline variant (rs4294451; 27% global minor allele frequency) located within this novel enhancer controls DPYD transcription and alters resistance to 5-FU. The variant genotype increases recruitment of the transcription factor CEBPB to the enhancer and alters the level of direct interactions between the enhancer and DPYD promoter. Our data provide insight into the regulatory mechanisms controlling sensitivity and resistance to 5-FU.

    19→## Introduction
    20→
    21→Therapeutic resistance has been reported for nearly all anti-cancer drugs ([Ramos et al., 2021](#bib47)), and as many as 90% of mortalities in cancer can be linked to drug resistance ([Mansoori et al., 2017](#bib34)). Enhancer-mediated regulation of gene expression has been increasingly implicated in multiple cancer-related processes, including resistance, response, and toxicity to cancer therapies ([Koutsi et al., 2022](#bib26); [Lauschke et al., 2019](#bib27)). However, the specific molecular mechanisms through which epigenetic changes drive these processes remain mostly elusive.
    22→
    23→Enhancers regulate gene expression by recruiting transcription factors (TFs) and subsequently interacting with the promoter region of a target gene to drive expression. The activity of enhancers can be affected by localized epigenetic state and genetic variations that affect TF binding. More than 90% of disease-associated genetic variants identified in genome-wide association studies (GWAS) lie in the non-coding portion of the genome ([Manolio et al., 2009](#bib33); [Maurano et al., 2012](#bib35)). Many of these GWAS variants map to putative enhancers ([Boix et al., 2021](#bib7)). Regardless, translating enhancer GWAS variants into disease mechanisms remains a largely unmet challenge ([Claringbould and Zaugg, 2021](#bib10)).
    24→
    25→In previous studies, we identified a *cis* expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) for the chemotherapeutic metabolism gene dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase enzyme (DPD, encoded by the *DPYD* gene) ([Etheridge et al., 2020](#bib13)). DPD is the initial and rate-defining enzyme for the conversion of the commonly used chemotherapeutic 5-FU into inactive metabolites. Hepatic DPD eliminates approximately 85% of circulating 5-FU within minutes of administration ([Sommadossi et al., 1982](#bib51); [Heggie et al., 1987](#bib19)), and deficiency of DPD is associated with severe (clinical grade ≥3) toxicity to 5-FU ([Amstutz et al., 2018](#bib4)). Clinical and preclinical studies have identified missense and splicing variants of *DPYD* that consistently correlate with reduced DPD function and increased 5-FU toxicity risk ([Amstutz et al., 2018](#bib4); [Lee et al., 2014](#bib28); [Shrestha et al., 2018](#bib50); [Offer et al., 2014b](#bib44); [Offer et al., 2013a](#bib41); [Offer et al., 2013b](#bib42); [Nie et al., 2017](#bib40)). However, these variants explain <20% of reported cases of grade ≥3 5 FU toxicities ([Amstutz et al., 2018](#bib4); [Meulendijks et al., 2015](#bib38)), and the mechanisms contributing to 5-FU resistance, and therefore means to overcome resistance, remain elusive.
    26→
    27→In the present study, we used human liver tissues and cellular models to identify and characterize a novel *cis*-enhancer element capable of modulating *DPYD* expression. We additionally provide mechanistic data demonstrating that transcription-factor driven expression of *DPYD* is dependent on allele status for a common germline genetic variant located within this enhancer, suggesting that the variant could be a valuable biomarker of 5-FU toxicity risk. Furthermore, the genotype-dependent regulation of DPD expression offers a potential mechanism for overcoming resistance in patients carrying the risk variant.

    29→## Results
    30→
    31→### Identification of DPYD enhancer regions
    32→
    33→Candidate *cis* enhancer regions for *DPYD* ([NM_000110.4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/NM_000110.4)) were identified using GeneHancer ([Fishilevich et al., 2017](#bib14)) and Ensembl Regulatory Build ([Zerbino et al., 2015](#bib55)) data mapped to GRCh37/hg19. We defined the upstream boundary of the target region using enhancer data supported by multiple methods and gene-enhancer links supported by multiple methods as implemented within GeneHancer (i.e. putative enhancer regions with ‘double-elite’ status; [Figure 1A](#fig1)). We then mapped annotated transcription factor binding sites in the target region using ChIP-seq data from the Ensembl Regulatory Build as an additional layer of evidence ([Figure 1A](#fig1)). In all, three candidate proximal upstream enhancer regions were identified, which will be referred to herein as E9, E16, and E20 based on the relevant location upstream of the *DPYD* transcription start site ([Figure 1A](#fig1)).

    43→CRISPR-interference (CRISPRi) and -activation (CRISPRa) were used to determine which of these regions were capable of regulating *DPYD* expression. For CRISPRi, we generated cell lines that stably expressed the Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) domain of Kox1 fused to nuclease-deficient Cas9 protein (dCas9-KRAB; [Yeo et al., 2018](#bib54)). For CRISPRa, we created cell lines that stably expressed dCas9 fused to the histone acetyltransferase E1A binding protein P300 (dCas9-P300; [Hilton et al., 2015](#bib21)). Six guide RNAs (gRNAs) were used to target dCas9 fusion proteins to each region of interest, and quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT-PCR) was used to monitor changes in *DPYD* expression following transfection.
    44→
    45→Targeting of dCas9-KRAB (CRISPRi) to the E9 region significantly decreased *DPYD* expression relative to control for four of six gRNAs ([Figure 1B](#fig1)). Targeting of dCas9-KRAB to E16 resulted in a significant decrease in *DPYD* expression for one of six gRNAs ([Figure 1C](#fig1)), and no significant reductions in *DPYD* expression were noted following transfection of gRNAs for E20 in dCas9-expressing cells; however, two gRNAs increased *DPYD* expression ([Figure 1D](#fig1)). For reciprocal CRISPRa experiments, targeting dCas9-P300 to E9 significantly increased *DPYD* expression for four of six gRNAs tested ([Figure 1E](#fig1)), whereas gRNAs specific to E16 and E20 did not elicit any significant changes in *DPYD* expression ([Figure 1F and G](#fig1)). Consistent with the results in HepG2 cells, CRISPRi and CRISPRa targeted to the E9 region significantly altered *DPYD* expression in HCT116 cells ([Figure 1—figure supplement 1A and D](#fig1s1)), whereas no significant changes were noted when targeted to E16 or E20 ([Figure 1—figure supplement 1B, C, E, F](#fig1s1)).
    46→
    47→To confirm that the expected epigenetic changes were induced at the E9 region, we performed chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) coupled with quantitative PCR (ChIP-qPCR) in dCas9-KRAB and -P300 expressing cells transfected with E9 gRNAs. As expected, targeting dCas9-KRAB to E9 resulted in a localized increase in H3K9me3 compared to controls, indicating a shift from active to inactive chromatin at E9 ([Figure 2A](#fig2); the primer nearest E9 is denoted by the gray box). Targeting of dCas-P300 to E9 caused changes consistent with epigenetic activation of the region, including a localized gain of H3K27ac-marked chromatin ([Figure 2B](#fig2)). Similar changes in localized histone modifications were noted when dCas9-KRAB and dCas9-P300 HCT116 cells were directed to E9 ([Figure 2C–D](#fig2)). Collectively, these results indicate that the E9 region might act as a *cis*-regulatory element modulating *DPYD* expression.

    57→### Identification of rs4294451 as a putative regulatory SNP within E9
    58→
    59→In an expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) genome-wide association study (GWAS) conducted in human liver specimens, Etheridge et al. previously identified three independent haplotype blocks that significantly associated with altered *DPYD* expression ([Etheridge et al., 2020](#bib13)). One of the blocks identified in that study spanned the E9 region, prompting us to investigate if genetic variants in E9 could potentially perturb *DPYD* regulation. Based on the biological role of enhancers, we postulated that a causal functional single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) could interfere with transcription factor binding. Within the Ensembl Regulatory Build ([Zerbino et al., 2015](#bib55)), we identified a region of approximately 150 bp showing evidence for binding by multiple transcription factors in HepG2 cells, but not in other cell types tested ([Figure 2—figure supplement 1](#fig2s1)). Within this region, we identified the variant rs4294451, which is a tag SNP for the previously identified eQTL block ([Etheridge et al., 2020](#bib13)), prompting us to hypothesize that this variant might be the causal eQTL SNP.
    60→
    61→To assess the contribution of rs4294451 alleles to *DPYD* regulation, we first performed reporter assays by cloning the *DPYD* promoter and E9 enhancer region containing either the reference or variant allele for rs4294451 (T or A, respectively) into a luciferase reporter vector ([Figure 3A](#fig3)). Reporter vectors containing the T-allele yielded significantly higher luciferase activity compared to vectors containing only the *DPYD* promoter (p=0.00054), whereas those containing the A allele within E9 showed a more modest increase (p=0.070, [Figure 3B](#fig3)). Directly comparing E9-containing reporters, vectors containing the T allele showed significantly higher activity than those with the A allele (p=0.046, [Figure 3B](#fig3)), suggesting that the variant could impact *DPYD* expression.

    71→### Impact of rs4294451 genotype on DPYD expression and regulation
    72→
    73→To directly characterize the impact of rs4294451 alleles on *DPYD* expression at the endogenous locus, we used CRISPR-mediated genome editing to create matched isogenic HCT116 knock-in cell models for each genotype. Consistent with the results from the reporter assay ([Figure 3B](#fig3)), cells homozygous for the A allele had significantly lower *DPYD* expression compared to cells that were homozygous for the T allele (*P*=0.0011, [Figure 3C](#fig3)). Heterozygous rs4294451 A/T cells displayed intermediate expression compared to cells with homozygous A/A and T/T genotypes (p=0.00013 and p=0.028, respectively; [Figure 3C](#fig3)). At the chromatin level, cells homozygous for the A allele showed reductions in H3K27ac and accumulation of H3K9me at E9 compared to cells that were homozygous or heterozygous for the T allele ([Figure 3D–E](#fig3)). This finding is consistent with the A allele being associated with a less active epigenetic state at E9. Cells carrying a heterozygous genotype for rs4294451 displayed an intermediate epigenetic state, which is consistent with one epigenetically active and one inactive copy of E9 ([Figure 3D–E](#fig3)). Similar epigenetic differences between rs4294451 genotypes were noted at E9 in liver specimens obtained from human donors ([Figure 3—figure supplement 1](#fig3s1)).

    75→### Allele-specific expression of DPYD transcripts
    76→
    77→To precisely determine the impact of rs4294451 alleles on *DPYD* expression in vivo, we measured allele-specific expression using reverse transcriptase digital droplet PCR (RT-ddPCR) using donor liver tissues. Because rs4294451 falls outside of the coding region, we used coding region variants as proxies to measure strand-specific expression. These coding variants consisted of *DPYD*-c.85T>C (rs1801265) and c.496A>G (rs2297595), both of which are in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with the rs4294451 T allele (c.85C: D′=0.92, R^2^=0.80; c.496G: D′=0.67, R^2^=0.21). Imperfect LD between rs4294451 and proxies allowed us to use samples that are heterozygous for the coding region SNP, but homozygous for rs429441 genotype, as an additional level of control. In samples measured using the c.85 genotype, the fractional abundance of the C-allele ranged from 0.542 to 0.602 in samples heterozygous for rs4294451 ([Figure 3F](#fig3)). Expressed in terms of relative expression, the C-allele was expressed at levels 18–51% higher than the T-allele (i.e. 0.542/0.458=1.18 and 0.602/0.398=1.51). In contrast, the sample that was homozygous for the rs4294451 T allele showed allelic expression at the c.85 locus that was indistinguishable from the DNA control ([Figure 3F](#fig3)), indicating equal expression of both transcripts. In measurements using c.496, a significant allelic-imbalance of 0.565 (CI: 0.546–0.584) in favor of the variant c.496 G-allele compared to the DNA control (CI: 0.488–0.511) was observed ([Figure 3G](#fig3)). This again indicated higher expression of the linked rs4294451 T allele. Notably, liver specimens that were homozygous at the rs4294451 locus again showed c.496 allelic expression that was indistinguishable from the DNA control ([Figure 3G](#fig3)). These data indicate that rs4294451 significantly affects the expression of *DPYD* in the liver.
